The origins of the settlement date back to the early medieval period, when a small farming community grew up around a crossing point on the river. By the twelfth century the village had acquired a parish church and a weekly market, and it appears in tax records from the reign of Edward I as a modest but established trading centre. The town suffered considerably during the Black Death, losing perhaps a third of its population, and recovery was slow over the following century. During the early modern period the local economy shifted towards wool production, and several of the timber-framed merchant houses that survive in the town centre date from this era of relative prosperity. The arrival of the turnpike road in the eighteenth century and the railway in 1846 transformed the town once again, bringing new industries and a rapid growth in population that continued into the twentieth century.
